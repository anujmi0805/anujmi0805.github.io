What is ROI?

ROI represents the percentage gain or loss relative to the amount invested. It helps you quickly gauge the performance of any investment in a simple, easy-to-understand metric.

Why ROI Matters

ROI is essential for comparing multiple investment opportunities. By expressing results as a percentage, it helps you make informed choices on where your money can work best for you over time.

Limitations

While ROI gives a snapshot of returns, it ignores the time factor. Two investments with the same ROI may have drastically different durations. For precise long-term comparisons, CAGR and XIRR provide deeper insights.

Example

For instance, investing ₹50,000 and receiving ₹75,000 after 5 years yields a 50% ROI. This simple calculation shows growth, but doesn’t reflect the yearly impact or compounding.

FAQs

Is ROI enough to judge an investment?
ROI gives a basic idea of gains, but it doesn’t consider the time invested or risk. Use it alongside CAGR or XIRR for a complete picture.
Can ROI be negative?
Yes. If the final value of an investment is lower than the initial investment, ROI will be negative, indicating a loss.
What is a good ROI?
A good ROI is one that surpasses inflation, aligns with your financial goals, and provides satisfactory returns on risk taken.
How often should ROI be calculated?
ROI can be calculated periodically—monthly, yearly, or at the end of the investment term—to track growth and performance trends.
Does ROI include dividends or interest?
ROI should include all cash flows, including dividends or interest, for an accurate representation of returns.
Can ROI be used to compare different asset classes?
Yes, ROI allows quick comparison between stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and other investments, but consider timeframes and risks as well.
How to improve ROI?
Increase returns by reinvesting gains, choosing higher-performing investments, or reducing costs and fees associated with your investments.
