CAGR Calculator

The CAGR calculator helps you understand the real annual growth rate of your investment by factoring in the power of compounding. Instead of just looking at how much your money has grown in total, CAGR tells you how efficiently your investment has grown every year. This makes it one of the most important tools for serious investors who want clarity, consistency, and better decision-making.

What is CAGR?

CAGR, or Compound Annual Growth Rate, represents the average yearly rate at which an investment grows over a specific period. It assumes that the investment grows at a steady rate every year, even though in reality markets fluctuate.

This concept is powerful because it converts uneven, volatile returns into a smooth annual percentage, allowing investors to compare different investments easily. Whether you are investing in stocks, mutual funds, or long-term assets, CAGR gives you a consistent way to measure performance.

How CAGR Works

CAGR works by taking the beginning value of your investment, the ending value, and the number of years between them. It then calculates the annual growth rate required to move from the starting value to the ending value.

For example, if your investment doubled in 5 years, CAGR will not simply say 100% growth. Instead, it will break that growth into a yearly percentage, giving you a much more realistic view of performance.

Why CAGR Matters for Investors

CAGR is widely used by professionals because it removes confusion caused by market volatility. Instead of reacting to short-term ups and downs, it helps you focus on long-term consistency.

It is especially useful when comparing multiple investment options. For example, if one investment grew faster but took longer, CAGR helps normalize the comparison.

Real Life Example

Imagine you invested ₹1,00,000 and after 5 years it became ₹2,00,000. At first glance, this looks like a 100% return. But CAGR tells you the annual growth rate required to reach that number, which gives a much deeper insight.

This allows you to compare it with other investments like SIPs, mutual funds, or even fixed deposits on a fair basis.
